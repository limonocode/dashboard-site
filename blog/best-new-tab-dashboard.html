New tab dashboards: the browser category nobody named

Updated July 2026

Read any “best tab manager 2026” roundup and you'll meet the same cast: OneTab, Session Buddy, Workona, Toby. Useful tools — but look at what they optimize: closing tabs and storing them out of sight. Research from CMU calls the result the “blackhole effect”: once something is out of view, it's effectively lost. Another study found bookmarks are barely ever retrieved unless they stay highly visible.

There is exactly one page in your browser that is always visible: the new tab. You open it dozens or hundreds of times a day. A Mozilla study logged over 12 million tab events from 27,000 participants — the new tab is the highest-frequency surface a browser has. And an experiment on new-tab recommendations showed its content measurably changes what people browse.

What makes a new tab dashboard different

  • Storage with a face. Your groups live on the page you already open — not in a popup you must remember to click.
  • Everything saved, not just tabs. Bookmarks, sessions, read-later articles and the posts you saved on social platforms belong together, because to you they're one thing: “stuff I meant to come back to”.
  • Resurfacing built in. If only ~16% of saves are ever retrieved, the dashboard's job is to bring the other 84% back: a forgotten gem of the day, “a year ago today”, snooze, random.

The honest landscape in 2026

Toby pioneered the idea of collections on a new tab, but reviewers consistently note it “only scratches the surface”, and its free tier now caps at 60 items with a mandatory account. Start-page tools like Momentum (2M+ users) proved people love a personal new tab — clock, greeting, focus — but store nothing. Tab managers store plenty but hide it.

Dashboard New Tab is our attempt to name the category properly: a local-first new tab where all your saves live — imported from nine platforms via official data exports, searchable with operators, kept tidy by rules and cleanup tools, personal with a clock, greeting and focus of the day, and free without an account. When it's something you'd rather keep out of sight — snooze it. When it's something you shouldn't forget — it will resurface on its own.

A save you never see again isn't saved. It's archived into oblivion — politely.
